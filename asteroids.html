ASTEROIDS

Crudely speaking, we can think of asteroids as the space rocks that weren't big enough to coalesce and form a planet.


WHAT IS AN ASTEROID?

  • Put simply, we can more or less think of any rock that orbits the Sun (but is too small to be considered a planet!) as an asteroid.
  • Most of them aren't large enough for their own gravity to force them into a round shape (like planets and dwarf planets) so they take on a variety of shapes and sizes, ranging from 1 meter across to hundreds of kilometers.
  • The largest is a body called Ceres, located in the Asteroid Belt between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter. Ceres (see third image in the slideshow) is classified as both an asteroid and a dwarf planet.
  • Asteroids are rocky or metallic bodies that orbit the Sun. They don't have an atmosphere because they don't have enough gravity to hold onto the gases that would form one in the first place.
  • It is asteroids that are responsible for most of the craters and impact sites we see around the inner, rocky planets, including Earth. When an asteroid hits the Earth, it is sometimes called a meterorite.

THE ASTEROID BELT

  • Most asteroids in the Solar System are found in what is called the Asteroid Belt, which is a relatively large cluster of asteroids that sit between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter.
  • Ceres is the only object in the asteroid belt that is also large enough to be considered a dwarf planet. All other dwarf planets are found beyond the planet Neptune, further out in the Solar System.

    • EXTRACT FROM BILL BRYSON'S BOOK

    The following is an extract from the book A Short History of Nearly Everything (Bill Bryson, 2003):

    "Asteroids, as most people know, are rocky objects orbiting in loose formation in a belt between Mars and Jupiter. In illustrations they are always show as existing in a jumble, but in fact the solar system is quite a roomy place and the average asteroid actually will be about one and a half million kilometres from its nearest neighbour. Nobody knows even approximately how many asteroids there are tumbling through space, but the number is thought to be probably not less than a billion. They are presumed to be a planet that never quite made it, owing to the unsettling gravitational pull of Jupiter, which kept - and keeps - them from coalescing.

    When asteroids were first detected in the 1800s - the very first was discovered on the first day of the century by a Sicilian named Giuseppi Piazzi - they were thought to be planets, and the first two were named Ceres and Pallas. It took some inspired deductions by the astronomer William Herschel to work out that they were nowhere near planet-sized but much smaller. He called them asteroids - Latin for 'starlike' - which was slightly unfortunate as they are not like stars at all. Sometimes now they are more accurately called planetoids."
