THE SUN

The Sun is the star of our Solar System. Everything else in the Solar System is gravitationally bound to the Sun. Without the Sun, there is no Solar System.


FACTS

WHAT COLOUR IS IT?

Remarkably, the answer is not yellow, as many people think. Light from the Sun is, in fact, white. This is because the Sun emits light over all wavelengths.

The reason it appears yellow, or sometimes red or orange, as seen from Earth, is that the Earth's atmosphere scatters other colours like blue, green and violet more easily, which leaves the "redder" colours behind.

ONE OF A KIND?

There is nothing special about the Sun when compared to other stars. What makes the Sun special is that it is the star that the Earth orbits, the only place in the Universe currently known to have life.

The Sun is the source of all life on Earth. Without it, life on Earth could not exist. Not just this, but it also provides just the right conditions that have allowed life on Earth to evolve over billions of years.

Scientists are actively looking for other planets in space that go round other stars and that could also support life. These are called exoplanets.

NOT SITTING STILL

Everything else in the Solar System orbits the Sun: all the planets (and their moons), dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, etc. But the Sun is not sitting still in the middle of space!

The Sun (and the Solar System as a whole, in fact) is orbitting the center of the Milky Way galaxy at a speed of 225 km/s.

Just as the planets orbit the Sun and are gravitationally bound to it, the Sun orbits and is gravitationally bound to, a supermassive black hole (called Sagittarius A*) at the galactic center.

The Sun also rotates on its axis (it is spinning) rapidly.

AN ANGRY BEAST

The Sun is alive and kicking. It can eject enormous quantities of matter at once in violent events known as coronal mass ejections (CMEs for short.)

The Sun is actually powered by nuclear fusion reactions that happen inside the core. Here, the temperatures and pressures are so large that the nuclei of atoms fuse (join) together, releasing colossal amounts of energy. Ultimately, all the energy that we get from the Sun on Earth started in the Sun's core.

A GODLY FIGURE

Many civilisations have worshipped the Sun throughout human history.

In ancient Greek mythology, Helios was the god of the Sun. The ancient Egypitians called their Sun god Ra, who was believed to be reborn every day as the Sun rose in the early morning sky.

IT WON'T LAST FOREVER

Just as the Sun was kind enough to allow life to exist on Earth, it will one day take it away forever.

The nuclear reactions inside the Sun's core will one day come to a stop. Then, the Sun will expand to 250 times its current size, swallowing up the inner planets including Earth.

All the liquid water on Earth's surface will have boiled away by the expanding Sun long before this happens.

Fortunately, there is a long wait before the nuclear reactions in the core stop, at least another 4 billion years!
