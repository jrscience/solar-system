MERCURY

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest planet in the Solar System. It has a solid, cratered surface, giving it a similar appearance to Earth's moon.


OVERVIEW



Sun   —   Mercury     —     Venus     —     Earth     —     Mars     —     Jupiter     —     Saturn     —     Uranus   —   Neptune

  • Closest planet to the Sun. First of the four rocky planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars)
  • Smallest and least massive planet in the Solar System
  • Heavily cratered surface, making it look similar to the Moon
  • No atmosphere, barren landscape
  • Little to no chance for life to survive there

FACTS

BURNT THEN FROZEN

Surface temperatures on Mercury swing wildly. During the day, when facing the Sun, the surface temperature reaches a staggering 430 °C. At night, when facing away from the Sun, the temperature drops to a chilly -180 °C. This is because Mercury has almost no atmosphere to hold on to the Sun's heat, so it escapes into space at night.

MESSENGER OF THE GODS

Mercury is named after the Greek messenger of the gods, Hermes. In Greek mythology, Hermes was the mediator between the gods and the mortals.

ITS DAY IS LONGER THAN ITS YEAR

Mercury's year (the time it takes for it to complete one orbit of the Sun) is 87.97 Earth days, but just 1 day on Mercury (the time from sunrise to sunset) is equivalent to 176 Earth days. Therefore, if you lived on Mercury, you would celebrate your birthday twice a day, every day!

ONLY TWO SPACECRAFTS HAVE EVER VISITED

Because it is so close to the Sun, Mercury is a difficult planet to visit. Only two spacecrafts have ever visited Mercury. During 1974 and 1975 Mariner 10 flew by three times, mapping much of the planet's surface. Then, in August 2004, the Messenger probe was launched from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station in Florida, United States and became the first spacecraft to visit since the mid 1970s.

WATER COULD EXIST IN THE FORM OF ICE

It is believed that craters located at the poles of Mercury could retain water ice. This is because the craters are cold and shadowy because light from the Sun cannot reach them.

IT HAS THE MOST CRATERS OF ANY PLANET IN THE SOLAR SYSTEM

Because it has almost no atmosphere, Mercury has nothing to protect it against meteor impacts. As a result, its surface has become heavily cratered, giving the planet a very similar look to our Moon.

LIFE ON MERCURY?

  • The variation in surface temperature between day and night on Mercury makes it extremely hostile to life as we know it.
  • The only possibility humans would have to colonise Mercury in the future would be its north and south poles. That's because light from the Sun never reaches them, so they are always dark, and the temperature never goes below -90 °C.
  • A base could one day be built within the craters at the poles and humans could be sent there to live. It would need to be self-sustaining, meaning that we would need to grow our own food, and harvest other essential resources such as water and energy in some way or another.
  • There is plenty of water available due to stores of water ice at the poles. It is estimated from data sent back by the MESSENGER probe that there could be between 100 billion and 1 trillion tons of water ice at both poles - more than enough to sustain even multiple bases on the planet!
  • Energy wouldn't be much of a problem either, as Mercury is nearly three times closer to the Sun than the Earth. Solar panels on Mercury would be far more efficient than they are on Earth and solar energy could be used to power a future civilisation there.
  • But life on Mercury would be a miserable one. The human body is not designed to live in such low gravity, causing our bones to weaken. The radiation would also be deadly because of Mercury's closeness to the Sun, presenting a range of health problems to us humans.
  • The scenery would also be very dull and depressing. You would permanently live in darkness.
  • Attempting to construct such a base would also be massively expensive. The chance for life on Mercury does not attract as much attention as other places in the Solar System and the money would be better spent trying to establish life elsewhere. Mars perhaps?
