URANUS

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun. Seen from space, it has a dull, pale blue appearance.


FACTS

A RINGED PLANET

Uranus (as well as Jupiter, Neptune and of course Saturn) also has rings circling the planet, though they are very faint and therefore far less visible than the rings of Saturn.

THE FIRST PLANET FOUND USING A TELESCOPE

Uranus was the first planet to be discovered through the use of a telescope, by Sir William Herschel in 1781.

Before then Uranus was not known as a planet to the ancients (it was observed - but believed to be a star!). This is mostly because of the fact that it appears very faintly in the night sky and is difficult to spot without a telescope because it is so far away from the Sun.

IT SPINS ON ITS SIDE

Except for Venus and Uranus, all the planets in the Solar System rotate (spin) about their own axes in an anti-clockwise direction. Venus is an oddity because it spins in a clockwise direction, while Uranus spins at a 98 degree angle to its axis! Thus it has been tipped over and is spinning "on its side".

Scientists believe that this is most likely due to a collision between Uranus and another large body, such as a planet, in the early days of the Solar System.

MOONS FROM LITERATURE

Uranus has 27 known moons, which are named after characters in works of literature written by William Shakespeare or Alexander Pope.

This is unusual, as most other moons in the Solar System, like the planets, are named after gods or other beings in ancient mythologies.

IT IS THE COLDEST PLANET IN THE SOLAR SYSTEM

The coldest recorded temperature on Uranus was a chilly -224° C - the coldest ever recorded on a planet in the Solar System.

Although the coldest temperature recorded was on Uranus, it is its more distant neighbour Neptune that holds the title for the coldest average temperature at around -200° C.

IT HAS ONLY BEEN VISITED BY ONE SPACECRAFT

Only one spacecraft - NASA's Voyager 2 probe - has flown past Uranus.

In January 1986, it came within 50,600 miles (81,500 km) of the planet's cloud tops.

In only six hours, Voyager 2 gathered and sent home information about Uranus.

Diameter 50,724 km This is the distance across Uranus, from one point on its surface to the opposite side.
Mass 14.5 Earths Uranus and Neptune (the ice giants) are less massive than Jupiter and Saturn (the gas giants) but still considerably more massive than the rocky planets.
Surface gravity 8.69 m/s^2 This is around 87% as strong as the Earth's gravity.
Escape velocity 21.3 km/s This is the minimum speed a rocket launched from the surface of Uranus would need in order to escape its gravity and enter into space.
Number of moons 27 Uranus has 27 known moons, which are named after various characters in works of literature.

LIFE ON URANUS?

  • When it comes to life elsewhere in the Solar System, unfortunately neither Uranus nor its moons are very promising, nor interesting. It is suspected that Miranda – the smallest moon of Uranus contains water ice but a lack of geological activity gives it a cold interior and little chance of having any liquid water.
  • Then there is Titania, the largest moon of Uranus. It has no atmosphere and an average surface temperature of -203°C, far too cold for life as we know it to be capable of surviving there. In addition, Titania only has around 4% the gravity as we have here on Earth. The possibility of humans colonising such a place in the Solar System is extremely unlikely. Especially when there are much more feasible and interesting places that are much closer to home such as Mars, Europa (moon of Jupiter) and Titan (moon of Saturn).
